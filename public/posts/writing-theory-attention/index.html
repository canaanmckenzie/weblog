Writing Theory and the Blue-Collar Life of Attention

Jan 07, 2025 · A note from the desk of a tired but hopeful writing coach.

A lot of writing theory gets described like its a set of sleek levers, a kind of white-board physics for prose, but what I see, at 2 a.m., is more like a factory shift. Attention is a working-class resource: it clocks in late, it takes smoke breaks, it gets injured on the job. The theory that is most humane is the theory that admits this and builds the sentence as a workplace with safety rails.

When a paragraph gives you a place to stand, to look, to breathe, it is not being precious; it is paying wages. A sentence that runs too long is not a sin, but it does require that the reader has the endurance to keep lifting. The tools of theory are less like laws and more like union contracts: the agreements by which writer and reader do not exploit one another.

So the cliché of clarity, which sounds like some antiseptic policy memo, is actually a kindness. It is a refusal to make the reader do extra labor just to locate the verb. You can still be weird, still be angular, still be honest about how the mind jerks around; you just don't leave the reader alone in the dark with a flashlight that has no batteries.

There is a way to write that feels like supervision, like the author staring over your shoulder. The better theory is a foreman who knows your name and lets you pick the music. It trusts you to make sense, and it leaves you a map.

Footnote: the map is not the territory. But if you keep telling yourself that, you end up with a territory that only you can navigate, which is how writing starts to smell like vanity. And if the theory is about anything at all, its about keeping the smell down.1

Footnotes

  1. I mean "smell" as in ego, not as in your actual room, which, if you are like me, is full of cold coffee and the faint sadness of old drafts. ↩
